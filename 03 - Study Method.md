# Study Method

> *"The goal is not to study more. The goal is to learn better."*

This document defines the learning methodology used throughout Engineering Mastery OS.

The objective is not to consume information quickly.

The objective is to transform information into long-term engineering knowledge and practical judgment.

Learning should be intentional, measurable, and continuously refined.

---

# Learning Philosophy

Learning software engineering is different from learning a programming language.

Programming languages can be memorized.

Engineering requires understanding.

For every topic studied, the objective is to answer:

* Why does it exist?
* What problem does it solve?
* How does it work?
* What are the tradeoffs?
* When should it be used?
* When should it be avoided?
* How does it behave at scale?

Until these questions can be answered confidently, the topic is not considered mastered.

---

# Learning Framework

Every study session follows the same lifecycle.

```text
Discover
    ↓
Understand
    ↓
Question
    ↓
Experiment
    ↓
Apply
    ↓
Reflect
    ↓
Document
    ↓
Teach
```

Knowledge becomes permanent only after it has been applied and explained.

---

# Study Sources Priority

Whenever possible, learning should prioritize high-quality sources.

Priority order:

1. Official Documentation
2. RFCs and Specifications
3. Books
4. Source Code
5. Engineering Blogs
6. Academic Papers
7. Conference Talks
8. Medium Articles
9. Dev.to Articles
10. AI Discussions

Community content should complement, not replace, primary sources.

---

# Weekly Study Routine

This project assumes one dedicated deep-learning session every Saturday.

A typical study session lasts between four and six hours.

Example schedule:

## Phase 1 — Review (30 minutes)

Review previous notes.

Answer:

* What did I learn last week?
* What do I still remember?
* What remains unclear?

Do not continue until previous knowledge has been refreshed.

---

## Phase 2 — Theory (60–90 minutes)

Study one focused topic.

Preferred resources:

* Book chapter
* Official documentation
* RFC
* Technical paper

The goal is understanding, not speed.

---

## Phase 3 — Discussion with Claude (45–60 minutes)

Instead of asking for a summary:

Discuss the topic.

Questions should include:

* Why does this exist?
* What alternatives exist?
* What are the tradeoffs?
* What mistakes do engineers commonly make?
* How does this behave in production?

The objective is critical thinking.

---

## Phase 4 — Hands-on Practice (60–120 minutes)

Build something.

Examples:

* Small prototype
* Algorithm
* API
* Refactor
* Benchmark
* Architecture exercise

Knowledge without implementation is incomplete.

---

## Phase 5 — Reflection (30 minutes)

Write notes.

Answer:

* What surprised me?
* What confused me?
* What assumptions changed?
* What would I explain differently now?

Reflection strengthens long-term retention.

---

## Phase 6 — Knowledge Capture (20 minutes)

Update Engineering Mastery OS.

Possible updates include:

* Reading Notes
* Knowledge Base
* Architecture Notes
* Decision Logs
* New Questions

The repository should continuously evolve.

# Reading Workflow

Every book chapter should follow this structure.

## Before Reading

Ask:

* What do I already know?
* What do I expect to learn?
* Which questions do I hope this chapter answers?

---

## During Reading

Highlight:

* New concepts
* Mental models
* Design decisions
* Tradeoffs
* Production examples

Avoid highlighting everything.

Capture only meaningful insights.

---

## After Reading

Create:

* Summary
* Questions
* Practical applications
* Connections with previous topics

Never finish a chapter without documenting it.

---

# Article Workflow

When reading a Medium article, engineering blog, or documentation page:

Do not simply summarize it.

Instead:

## Step 1

Summarize the article.

---

## Step 2

Extract the main concepts.

---

## Step 3

Evaluate credibility.

Questions:

* Is this opinion?
* Is this evidence?
* Does official documentation support it?
* Under which assumptions is this true?

---

## Step 4

Compare with existing knowledge.

Does it confirm?

Does it contradict?

Does it introduce something new?

---

## Step 5

Identify practical applications.

How could this improve:

* Current work
* Personal projects
* Architecture decisions
* Performance
* Code quality

---

## Step 6

Create action items.

Examples:

* Try this pattern.
* Read the referenced RFC.
* Build a prototype.
* Benchmark alternatives.

Reading should produce action.

---

# Learning by Building

Every major topic should produce something tangible.

Possible outputs:

* Mini project
* Blog post
* Architecture diagram
* Source code
* ADR
* Technical presentation
* Cheat sheet
* Teaching session

If nothing was created, learning is probably incomplete.

---

# Learning by Teaching

One of the strongest indicators of mastery is teaching.

After studying a topic, attempt to explain it as if teaching another engineer.

Claude should actively test my explanations.

If I cannot explain something clearly, I probably do not understand it well enough.

---

# Active Recall

Avoid passive review.

Instead:

Close the book.

Close the documentation.

Attempt to answer questions from memory.

Examples:

* Explain the Event Loop.
* Explain MVCC.
* Explain CAP Theorem.
* Explain React reconciliation.

The brain remembers what it retrieves.

---

# Spaced Repetition

Important concepts should be revisited.

Suggested intervals:

* Next day
* One week
* One month
* Three months
* Six months

Engineering is cumulative.

Forgetting fundamentals slows future learning.

---

# Deliberate Practice

Focus on weaknesses.

Avoid repeatedly studying topics that already feel comfortable.

Instead:

* Strengthen weak areas.
* Solve harder problems.
* Increase complexity gradually.

Growth happens outside the comfort zone.

---

# Monthly Review

At the end of every month:

Review:

* Books completed
* Topics mastered
* Projects completed
* Architecture exercises
* Code reviews
* Interview performance

Then answer:

* What improved?
* What remained difficult?
* What should change next month?

---

# Yearly Review

Every year revisit:

* Goals
* Learning priorities
* Career objectives
* Knowledge gaps
* Engineering strengths

Update the roadmap accordingly.

The learning system should evolve together with my career.

---

# Role of Claude

Claude is not only an assistant.

Claude acts as:

* Mentor
* Teacher
* Staff Engineer
* Software Architect
* Technical Reviewer
* Interviewer
* Research Partner

Claude should adapt explanations to my experience.

Avoid beginner-level explanations unless explicitly requested.

Whenever possible:

* Ask questions before giving answers.
* Encourage reasoning.
* Identify misconceptions.
* Recommend deeper exploration.
* Connect concepts across disciplines.

---

# Final Principle

Never optimize for finishing topics.

Always optimize for understanding them.

Mastery is measured by engineering judgment, not by the number of completed lessons.
