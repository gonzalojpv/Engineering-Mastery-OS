# Reading Notes

> *"Books are conversations with experienced engineers. Reading Notes are my side of the conversation."*

This document defines how books, articles, research papers, documentation, and technical resources should be studied inside Engineering Mastery OS.

The goal is not to finish books.

The goal is to extract durable engineering knowledge.

Every reading session should produce understanding, questions, insights, and practical actions.

---

# Reading Philosophy

Reading is an active process.

A book is not a collection of facts.

It is a collection of ideas.

My responsibility is not to remember every page.

My responsibility is to:

* Understand the author's reasoning.
* Challenge assumptions.
* Compare ideas.
* Connect concepts.
* Apply what I learn.
* Decide whether I agree or disagree.

Every reading session should improve my engineering judgment.

---

# Reading Workflow

Every resource follows the same process.

```text
Choose Resource
        ↓
Define Learning Goal
        ↓
Read Actively
        ↓
Discuss with Claude
        ↓
Extract Knowledge
        ↓
Connect Ideas
        ↓
Apply
        ↓
Document
        ↓
Review Later
```

Reading without reflection is incomplete.

---

# Reading Priorities

When multiple learning resources exist, prioritize them in the following order:

1. Official Documentation
2. RFCs & Specifications
3. Books
4. Academic Papers
5. Engineering Blogs
6. Conference Talks
7. Source Code
8. Medium Articles
9. Community Discussions
10. AI Conversations

Community content should expand understanding, not replace foundational sources.

---

# Before Reading

Before starting any chapter or article, answer the following questions.

## Why am I reading this?

Define a clear learning objective.

Examples:

* Improve API design.
* Understand PostgreSQL indexes.
* Learn distributed systems.
* Improve testing strategies.

Reading without intention creates shallow knowledge.

---

## What do I already know?

Write down current understanding.

This creates a baseline for measuring progress.

---

## What questions do I want answered?

Examples:

Why does this architecture exist?

Why is this approach preferred?

Which tradeoffs are involved?

When should I avoid it?

---

# During Reading

Do not highlight everything.

Capture only information that changes your understanding.

Focus on:

* Mental models.
* Design decisions.
* Tradeoffs.
* Engineering principles.
* Production examples.
* Unexpected insights.

Whenever possible, stop reading and explain the concept in your own words before continuing.

---

# After Reading

Every reading session should produce structured notes.

---

## Resource

Book, article, documentation page, paper, or video.

---

## Chapter or Section

Document exactly what was studied.

---

## Summary

Write a concise explanation using your own words.

Avoid copying text.

Understanding is demonstrated through explanation.

---

## Key Concepts

List the most important ideas introduced.

Examples:

* MVCC
* Event Loop
* Dependency Injection
* Idempotency
* Backpressure

---

## Why It Matters

Describe why the concept exists.

Which engineering problem does it solve?

---

## Tradeoffs

Document:

Advantages

Disadvantages

Complexity

Operational costs

Performance considerations

Maintainability

---

## Questions

Capture everything that remains unclear.

Unanswered questions become future study topics.

---

## Connections

Relate the concept to previous knowledge.

Examples:

Event Loop

↓

Concurrency

↓

Streams

↓

Queues

↓

Distributed Systems

Knowledge should become a connected graph rather than isolated notes.

---

## Practical Applications

Where could this knowledge improve:

* Current work
* Personal projects
* Architecture decisions
* Performance
* Code quality

Learning becomes valuable when it influences decisions.

---

## Action Items

Examples:

Build a prototype.

Read the RFC.

Review source code.

Benchmark two approaches.

Watch a conference talk.

Refactor an existing project.

Every reading session should end with action.

---

## Reflection

What changed in my thinking?

Did this confirm previous beliefs?

Did it challenge assumptions?

Did it reveal knowledge gaps?

Reflection transforms information into experience.

---

# Reading with Claude

Claude should never simply summarize a book or article.

Instead, Claude should:

* Explain difficult concepts.
* Challenge my understanding.
* Ask questions.
* Compare alternative viewpoints.
* Recommend additional resources.
* Relate ideas to previous knowledge.
* Suggest practical experiments.
* Identify misconceptions.
* Connect theory with production experience.

Claude should act as a technical mentor, not as a summarization tool.

---

# Medium & Engineering Blogs

When sharing a Medium article or engineering blog, Claude should analyze it using the following framework.

## Summary

What is the central idea?

---

## Problem

Which engineering problem does the article attempt to solve?

---

## Evidence

Does the author provide evidence?

Benchmarks?

Production examples?

Research?

Or is it primarily opinion?

---

## Tradeoffs

Which tradeoffs are discussed?

Which tradeoffs are ignored?

---

## Comparison

Compare the article with:

* Official documentation.
* Books.
* RFCs.
* Previous notes.
* Industry best practices.

---

## Practical Value

Can this idea be applied?

If so:

Where?

Why?

When?

---

## Follow-up Learning

Recommend:

* Books.
* RFCs.
* Documentation.
* Source code.
* Conference talks.
* Related concepts.

Every article should expand the learning roadmap.

Do not read to finish books.

Every page should improve not only what I know, but how I reason, design, and make technical decisions.
