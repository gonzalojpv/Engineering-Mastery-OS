# Progress

> *"Progress is measured by improved engineering judgment, not by completed checklists."*

This document tracks long-term growth throughout Engineering Mastery OS.

The objective is not to count books, courses, or hours.

The objective is to measure meaningful improvements in engineering capability.

Progress should be supported by evidence.

Every milestone should demonstrate deeper understanding, stronger decision-making, and better engineering practices.

---

# Progress Philosophy

Growth in software engineering is cumulative.

Knowledge compounds over time.

The most meaningful indicators of progress are not completion metrics but changes in how problems are understood, discussed, designed, and solved.

Every review of this document should answer one question:

**Am I becoming a better engineer than I was last month?**

# Annual Review

At the end of every year answer:

How has my engineering thinking changed?

Which competencies improved the most?

Which books changed my perspective?

Which projects had the greatest impact?

What mistakes became valuable lessons?

The annual review is an opportunity to recalibrate the roadmap.

# Evidence of Mastery

Progress should be demonstrated through evidence.

Examples include:

Technical presentations.

Blog articles.

Conference talks.

Code reviews.

Open source contributions.

Teaching sessions.

---

# Role of Claude

Claude should regularly review this document and help me evaluate my growth objectively.

Responsibilities include:

Identify strengths.

Highlight knowledge gaps.

Recommend new priorities.

Suggest books and resources.

Adjust the roadmap.

Challenge complacency.

Celebrate meaningful progress.

Encourage long-term consistency.

Progress reviews should be honest, evidence-based, and focused on continuous improvement.

---

# Guiding Questions

At any point in time I should be able to answer:

What can I do today that I could not do six months ago?

Which engineering decisions am I making with greater confidence?

Which concepts have become intuitive?

What still feels uncomfortable?

Where should I invest my next 100 hours of focused learning?

These questions are more valuable than any numerical score.

---

# Final Principle

Engineering mastery is not a destination.

It is the result of thousands of small improvements accumulated over years.

This document exists to make those improvements visible, measurable, and meaningful.

Every update should remind me that becoming a great engineer is not about moving faster than others.

It is about continuously becoming better than my previous self.
